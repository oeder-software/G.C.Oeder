Theory of Widow Funds and Relief Institutions in General,
by Georg Christian Oeder*.

*) D. M. April 79. 349–65. “More on Widow Funds.”
I. It is not only advisable, I said above (page 355), but necessary—and also not difficult—to make the calculations occurring in a relief institution comprehensible to any intelligent reader. When stripped of all algebra, the theories take on a very simple appearance; and now I intend to attempt such a comprehensible presentation of the true theory of widow funds.
II. Certainly, it is necessary—in a matter so intimately tied to the public good, which concerns the welfare of wives and their husbands, of children and their fathers, and of entire families by the hundreds and thousands, and where a mistaken path followed for long can lead to the loss of barrels of gold, even millions—certainly, it is necessary that at least the directors of such institutions may see with their own enlightened eyes and act out of deep conviction. To doubt the will and desire for conviction would be contrary to the fairness and respect owed to esteemed and well-meaning men; and I therefore believe that the only thing lacking has been the proper degree of comprehensibility to produce widespread conviction and consensus about the true theory—which, in essence, can only be one. This would put an end to the many disputes that have often escalated into quarrels, prevent erroneous plans at their origin, and halt the progress of misguided measures.
III. The actual foundation for everything is a mortality or decrement table—that is, a table from which one can see the degree to which a certain number of people born at the same time gradually disappear from the world. And among the various tables of this kind, we naturally choose, here in northern Germany, the one by Süßmilch, as it appears in the new edition, volume 2, pages 319–322.
IV. Upon this foundation we build using certain teachings and aids from arithmetic. Namely:Firstly and most importantly, we need a table showing the growth of a capital sum when interest is compounded—interest upon interest—and continually added back to the principal.Secondly, for explanation, we need a table showing the present value of an upcoming periodic payment.Thirdly, for convenience and to ease the calculations, we make use of logarithmic tables.
V. When what is strictly mathematical in the matter is either presupposed or set aside, and separated from the reasoning about the decrement table and the instruction on how to draw the proper conclusions and inductions from it—when all this is illustrated immediately by a single example, especially in a matter where each individual case can serve as instruction for all—then everything becomes comprehensible even without special mathematical knowledge. One finds that once the reasoning has been clarified, the calculation itself is easier to grasp than it might seem to someone unaccustomed to algebraic formulas. And while it may be lengthy and laborious, it consists entirely of purely mechanical operations.

So first, a few words about the arithmetic tools that must be presupposed.
VI. As for the use of logarithmic tables, the great ease they provide becomes immediately evident, since they transform multiplication into addition and division into subtraction. In the calculations required for establishing a relief institution, one deals with numbers of 4 to 6 digits, which must be multiplied or divided by one another.

Moreover, logarithms are indispensable for constructing the two types of discount tables that will be explained shortly.

VII. The following first auxiliary table is used to discount a distant single sum. This table, calculated at an interest rate of 2 percent, compounded semiannually, shows the growth of the capital through the addition of interest. If one multiplies any given capital by a number from this table, one finds the growth of the assumed capital over the period shown beside it in the table. Conversely, if one divides the given capital by the same number, one obtains its present value—provided it is a capital sum that becomes due only after the indicated time.

Now to the reasoning regarding the decrement table.
IX. When applying a (chosen) decrement table to a pension institution and deriving conclusions from the table, it is above all necessary to guard against a mistaken line of thought, which one can easily and unknowingly fall into.Namely, one might be tempted to assess and determine the risk involved with an upcoming pension obligation for a pensioner based on the average remaining lifetime.

For example, if we see from the decrement table that of a given number of 30-year-old persons, half will have died after 28 full years and about 15/16 of a year, and the other half will still be alive, we may rightly conclude that, on average, 30-year-olds live another 28 15/16 years.Or if we assume 10,000 30-year-olds and sum, year by year up to the 50th year, the number of persons still alive each year, and that sum is 168,339, and we divide it by 10,000, we may likewise say that 30-year-olds, on their way to the end of the 50th year, on average reach 16 8/10 years—assuming we disregard the partial time lived by those who die between two given terms.

But if we then go on to say: "Therefore, for every 30-year-old person who requests a life pension, we must be prepared to pay for 28 15/16 years," or: "For a person of that age who is to be pensioned until the completion of their 50th year, we must expect to pay for 16 8/10 years"—and if we then further say: "Therefore, in the first case we require the value of a pension paid over 28 15/16 years, and in the second case the value of a pension paid over 16 8/10 years,"—then our reasoning is incorrect, and we are demanding too much.
X. Nevertheless, this line of reasoning appears plausible, and one falls into it quite easily. The writers on the subject might well have taken greater care to more deliberately counter and guard against it.
XI. One must, therefore, carefully consider the nature of a periodic, uniform annuity—that is, although the payments are numerically equal, they are unequal in value with respect to the present, and decrease in cash value the further they lie in the future. For example, 10 rigsdalers to be received after one year are worth less than 10 rigsdalers received immediately; 10 rigsdalers due in two years are worth even less, and so on.

If we now divide the pension period of a pensioner A into two equal halves, the value of the pensions in the latter half is less than the value of those in the earlier half.If two other persons, B and C, receive the same pension, but B for a shorter time than A and C for an equally longer time than A, then the combined value of B’s and C’s pensions is not twice the value of A’s, but slightly less.

If we then add two more persons, D and E, whose pension periods lie even further away from the midpoint—i.e., A’s pension time—and we take all five persons A, B, C, D, and E, calculate the total value of their pension benefits, and divide that by five, then this fifth part is not equal to the value of the pension at the midpoint (i.e., A’s pension), but somewhat less.

And if we imagine 10,000 people, the same holds true. In short: the average total expenditure on all pensioners does not correspond exactly to the average pension duration; rather, this average total expenditure is noticeably less than the value of a pension paid over the average time span.

XVI.The sum of these discounted values (discounts) is 150,470, and this represents the value of the annual pensions that are paid in yearly terms to the persons still alive at each term, drawn from the original group of 10,000, until their final death.

That is: 150,470 annual pensions, thought of as paid all at once in present value, are equivalent to all the annual pensions that numerically occur over time.The average is 15.047 annual pensions, representing the risk associated with each individual 30-year-old.
XVII.If the pension is not to be fully lifelong, but instead ends at some earlier point before the ultimate limit of human life, then the discount values from XV are summed only up to that predetermined end point.

For example, if the pension is to cease after 20 years—that is, at the completion of the pensioner’s 50th year—then only the first 20 discount values are added.Their sum is 117,149, and the average is 11.7149,so the risk for a 30-year-old person who is to receive an annual pension up to the end of their 50th year is 11.7149 / 10,000 annual pensions.
XVIII.If the pension is to begin only after a certain deferral period, one subtracts the value of the pension during the deferral period from the total value of the lifelong pension.The remainder is the value or risk for the remaining period—from the end of deferral to the end of life.

From 15.047 subtract 11.7149, leaving 3.3321 annual pensions as the risk for a 30-year-old person who is to receive an annual pension only beginning upon reaching their 50th year, continuing until death.

XXI. We know from section XIV how many individuals of type A (aged 30) remain at the end of each year, and from XX how many individuals of type B (aged 40) remain —but not all remain paired; some lose their spouse and live a while unpaired.
To determine how many intact couples are still living together at each point in time, we reason as follows:
Every A and every B originally had a spouse.
As the entire cohort of A declines, and likewise B, so too does the portion of those initially paired.
For example, after one year, suppose 9863 A and 9811 B are still alive. Then we say:
Just as 9811 of 10,000 B remain, so of the B’s originally paired with those 9863 A’s, about 9677 remain.
Or inversely:
Just as 9863 of 10,000 A remain, then of the A’s originally paired with the 9811 B’s, 9677 remain.
So in both cases, the proportion is:
 10,000 : 9863 = 9811 : 9677
Thus, for each year, we multiply the corresponding A and B values from tables XIV and XX, and divide the result by 10,000 to find the remaining joint couples.

XXVII.So far, only those individuals who are exactly alive at the end of each year have been considered, and therefore it is assumed when applying the results to a pension institution that pensions are paid once yearly at the year’s end to those still living.

However, often semi-annual payments and payments up to the exact day of death are desired by the interested parties.Funeral expenses become necessary immediately upon death,and for good reasons, institutions often require a prepayment (prenumeration) when calculating contributions.

To accommodate such wishes fairly,the mortality (decrement) table must be extended to half-year terms,and account must be taken of those who die between two terms and survive a portion of the interval.

XXXI.Now a third circumstance concerning the contribution arises. Namely, if the pension of a dependent pensioner is to begin immediately from the death day of the provider, the institution must also be secured regarding the punctual payment of contributions up to the moment of separation of coexistence. This security exists for the pensioner, but not so easily for the provider who has lost their pensioner and has nothing to lose by omitting payment. The safest is therefore to have the contribution prepaid from term to term. Accordingly, the number of contributors increases by the fully present initial number of 10,000 individuals, and the value of coexistence increases by this contribution for a half-year term. Thus, the increased value of coexistence found in XXIX, 11.2678, is further increased by 0.5, making it 11.7678, and 4.2069 divided by 11.7678 yields the quotient 0.35749.
XXXII.For the cases described in XVII and XVIII and similar, where reference is made to a goal within the utmost limit of life, one finds the proportional increase caused by the mentioned circumstances by saying that just as the value found purely from the decrement table for existence until the end of life is increased to the augmented value of precisely this existence, so the tabular value of existence up to the stated goal is increased to the same value; just as 15.047 in XVI to 15.4747 in XXIX, so 11.7149 in XVII and XVIII to 12.048, the value of existence of the 30-year-old up to completion of their 50th year.If from 15.4747 in XVIII one subtracts 12.048, the remainder is 3.4267, the value of the further existence of the 30-year-old person after passing their 50th year.
XXXIII.The work of calculating complete tables for a pension institution, as shown from XIV to XXVI, is already extensive and laborious, and it would become exceedingly cumbersome if one, considering the circumstances mentioned in XXVII, were to treat all cases as thoroughly as shown in XXVIII to XXXI. One would rather avoid involving all these mentioned circumstances everywhere and simply accept that income and expenditure terms are annual, and that anyone who dies between two terms is not counted, considered, or claimed from, but only the pensioner or contributor who is alive exactly at the term receives a pension or pays a contribution.

XXXV.Namely, when one carries out the calculation of the amount of the influence that the aforementioned circumstances have, according to the instructions in §§ XXVII–XXXI, in a detailed manner for various cases and higher and lower stages of life, one finds that this amount of increase resulting from these circumstances mostly comes out to about one. This arises because although in cases of a lower stage of life there are more discounted sums to accumulate, on the other hand, in cases of a higher stage, partly the decrease of persons from term to term becomes more significant, and partly the numbers lose less upon discounting.
XXXVI.It is understood upon closer consideration of the extensive calculus required for complete tables, and thus hardly needs to be mentioned, that it would be too far-reaching if the detailed calculus should extend to every combination of different ages. Calculations for simple life annuities are reasonably done for every year of human age, but for pensions of dependent pensioners and the like, it can be sufficient to make determinations for cases in five-year intervals and fill in intermediate cases arithmetically by interpolation.

For example, if the value of the pension for case P 30 V 40 and for case P 30 V 45 is found by calculation according to the rule, the difference between these two quantities is divided into five parts, and for the intermediate cases P 30 V 41, P 30 V 42, P 30 V 43, P 30 V 44, one such fifth part is added each time.
XXXVII.Finally, I want to remind that when I say, as I stated at the beginning in § III, that in our northern Germany, among the various decrement tables, we naturally choose the Süßmilch table, I by no means want to deny that this table, like all others existing with various authors of different nations, may need and be capable of improvements. I am by no means inclined to discourage or dissuade efforts to improve this table as unnecessary or superfluous. Such tables are the foundation of all kinds of support institutions, and thus their lesser or greater perfection is of great importance.

Merely mentioning the difference of gender: if one considers that more boys than girls are born, yet the majority of boys are lost before puberty, that all censuses show a surplus (of about 5%) of females over males, and although the years of childbearing and the cessation of menstruation undoubtedly bring hardships for the female sex — though in our times with better obstetrics and better healing methods generally, demonstrably far less than in the times of our mothers and grandmothers — one cannot well conceal that one and the same table can hardly fit both sexes equally well through all stages and epochs of human life.

The higher degree of mortality among boys deserves consideration in a child welfare fund, while the hardship of childbearing for women, and the indisputably greater longevity of matrons after successfully overcoming the critical period, undoubtedly deserve attention in a widow’s fund: it only depends on being able to say and specify something definite upon which a calculation can be based.
XXXVIII.Meanwhile, one must not imagine the imperfection of the Süßmilch table, like all other decrement tables, to be too great, nor act as if nothing has yet been done, nor deny the possibility of thorough support institutions everywhere. Certainly, the Süßmilch table, as it is, is usable; the experience presented in this museum in the month of April speaks in favor of the correctly derived inductions from this table and proves them tangibly, and the discussion is really only about how one can provide further perfection to an already good matter.

It does not at all follow that if we now come to improvements of the Süßmilch table, all our previous tables must be recalculated; rather, shorter remedies might be found — for example, against the greater longevity of mature women, advice can be given simply by assuming such women at admission to be somewhat younger than they actually are, without changing the tables, and one only needs a modification indicated by proven experience to specify what the Süßmilch table lacks with regard to these mature women, to make this somewhat definite.

For strict accuracy, it would be necessary, instead of the single decrement table derived from combined experience of both sexes, to have two such tables, one for one sex and the other for the other sex, and then to perform all calculations twice, indeed in cases where dependent pensioners are concerned, even three times: once when both provider and pensioner are male, second when both are female, and third when they are of different sexes. I wish for these two decrement tables, but I believe that when they eventually exist, one will not find the difference in results so great that one is inevitably forced to the multiple calculations, but rather that shorter ways will be found.
XXXIX.The certain way to arrive at a perfect decrement table, whose perfection would also be increased by the influence of all local circumstances being reflected in it, would be that by official decree some thousands of children, born in one, or a few, at most three consecutive years in a city or province, for example the city of Hamburg and its territories, would be registered, and then sustained attention would follow them throughout their entire lives, to record the departure of each from the world, while occasionally various other significant circumstances could also be noted simultaneously.

Our era would indeed not yet enjoy the benefit of such an arrangement itself, but it would be a tree planted for posterity; must not trees be planted by ancestors for the benefit of descendants? And yet even our time could partially benefit from the experience in respect to widow’s funds.
