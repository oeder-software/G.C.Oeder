Reminder towards XXXIII above p. 147 ff.



However, the population figure of the Osnabrück bishopric given in Mr. Möser’s Phantasien required the correction I noted; and the excellent gentleman kindly accepted and substantiated this correction. The miles of which the aforementioned bishopric supposedly contains only 28 according to that source are Osnabrück local square miles, which relate to geographical square miles as 289 to 144. Accordingly, the size of the Osnabrück bishopric is a little over 56 geographical square miles; and its population of 116,664 (assuming no double counting of individuals among the categories listed for the place) distributed over this area would yield at most 2,083 people per geographical square mile.

A population density of more than 4,000 per square mile would hardly be found in any part of Germany, excluding the immediate surroundings of large cities and marshland regions. Oldenburg also has significant linen and yarn manufacturing—for example, in the village of Zetel over 300 looms—and of its area (45½ square miles), about 13 square miles are marshland, where the population density averages about 3,300 and in some places approaches 4,000 people per square mile.

Therefore, it remains unlikely that other lands in the same district would be so much more densely populated than Oldenburg, namely by more than double. However, the reason for my objection to the purported population of Osnabrück was not, as the author of the essay on the population of the County of Ravensberg attributes to me, the smaller population of Oldenburg, which would make a higher population for Osnabrück impossible; rather, it was the larger area of Osnabrück, namely 56 miles instead of 28, for which no one can dispute that 4,166 people per mile cannot be correct, but rather only about 2,083 people per mile.

— Oeder
