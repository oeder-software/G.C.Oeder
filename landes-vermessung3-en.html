Notice of the Local Survey of the Land.


Third Section.


For the geodetic labours, that is to say, the proper measuring and delineation of the country itself, the plane table is employed. This instrument attains its true usefulness and reliability through the telescope, of which mention has been made above, and which may be mounted upon the diopters hollowed out for this purpose. Behind the ocular glass are stretched, as in the telescopes of the geographical instrument, two fine threads intersecting one another at right angles; and upon the tube there is affixed a spirit level, together with a pin, in order to bring the tube into a horizontal position, and one of the intersecting threads at the glass into a vertical one. Likewise, one of the diopters may be raised, so as to incline the tube, and thus to sight somewhat above and below the horizon. It is readily intelligible that by this means one may sight more distinctly and sharply upon an object which is brought into the point of intersection of the threads, than through the slits of the ordinary diopters, according to the guidance of the lamella which covers far too much of the object. We therefore do indeed also employ the diopter ruler with the customary slit diopters for objects at close range; but for distant objects, and wherever accuracy is required, the sighting is performed through the tube.

By such sharp sighting the geodetist thus also finds his way through districts and tracts where the view is confined and frequently interrupted, where there is no room for intersections, and where one must proceed only with simple lines, upon whose exact direction everything then depends; and he has the satisfaction, when he emerges from such a district, for example a forest tract, once more into the open field, and again brings into view the objects laid down upon the plane table for his guidance, that, in sighting upon such objects, the lines of vision coincide. The same satisfaction he likewise experiences with those objects laid down upon the plane table by means of intersections, which occurs everywhere where opportunity for it and space upon the horizon are afforded, in that, upon the subsequent measurement of the distances — which also, by way of trial, is often carried out upon a part of them — the measure taken from the paper according to the scale agrees exactly with the measure taken in the field.

This sharp sighting is then also that by which the use of the plane table for the measurement of angles is rendered possible. A considerable advantage! for a geographical instrument for the measuring of angles is in itself costly, and for the geodetist to carry it about with him is, besides the expense of transport, also burdensome; whereas now he can, with his measuring table, even in this respect, so far as it may become necessary to him, sufficiently provide for himself.

Provided with such a means for sharp sighting, and guided by the trigonometrically determined objects lying visibly before his eyes in the field, and laid down upon the plane-table sheet — upon which the operations are now carried out, and which is to be filled in — according to the calculated distances from the established base lines, the assumed meridian and perpendicular, the geodetist now proceeds, without compass, and more correctly oriented than could ever be achieved by means of a compass, upon his course with a sure step; and the map of the district, which finds room upon his sheet, is completed immediately in the field, without any rough draft, so that between the sheet cut off from the plane table and the subsequent fair copy there is no other difference than that which cleanliness in handling entails. At every step he takes, at every station which he occupies in the progress of his operations, at every new object which he brings upon the paper, the geodetist has the verification at hand; for he need only, with the position of his plane table unchanged, apply his diopter ruler with the tube to the point representing the station at which he is situated, and to any chosen point among the trigonometrical points previously laid down upon his plane table, and observe whether the trigonometrically determined point in the field falls into the axis of his tube; and if this occurs, he may be certain that his new station has been correctly laid down upon the paper.

The charts to be prepared are of three kinds: first, a chart of the entire country, according to the scale of 1 decimal inch of a Rhenish foot to 16,000 Rhenish feet, which will be engraved in copper; secondly, charts of the bailiwicks, according to the scale of 1 inch to 2,000 feet, of which three fair copies are to be prepared, for the Ducal Cabinet, for the Ducal Chamber, and for the district office to which each bailiwick belongs; thirdly, special charts of the bailiwicks, according to the scale of 1 inch to 400 feet, of which two fair copies are to be prepared, for the Chamber and for the district office to which the bailiwick belongs.

The General Chart will measure, from north to south, approximately 19 Rhenish decimal inches, and from east to west approximately 18 inches. It can, of course, not be prepared earlier than at the conclusion of all geodetic labours.

In the bailiwick charts every patch or parcel of land which, in nature or in the field, by ditch, fence or hedge, roads, or otherwise by any separation striking to the eye, presents itself as a distinct piece or small piece of land, is likewise so represented; further divisions, however, which are merely conventional and rest upon no visible marks, are passed over in these charts. All human dwellings, ancillary buildings together with the principal buildings, are drawn in; likewise the use (the use current at the time when the geodetist is working in each place) of every piece or small piece of land is expressed by means of suitable coloured signatures, so that these bailiwick charts afford a view of the whole, such as one might wish to have when travelling over the country in a Montgolfière, and one sees how people dwell beneath and beside one another, how cultivation is constituted according to degree and kind, how much land remains uncultivated, and what further use might yet be made of it. The chart speaks to the eye, and lays directly before it the answer to a hundred questions, the oral or written answering of which is always prolix.

In the special charts, at each corner of every patch or small patch of land, or parcel, a letter of the capital Latin alphabet is placed, which is thus common to several patches adjoining one another at that corner. By means of these letters each patch may therefore be designated, and whatever is to be said of it may be expressed in an intelligible manner. Each chart, all of which are drawn upon so-called elephant paper in half sheets, in such a way that the margins of the sheet indicate the cardinal directions, is divided into four quarters; and in a separate copy of the bailiwick chart the extent of each special chart and their mutual connection are represented, so that one may see therein in which special chart the piece of land in question must be sought. The bailiwick charts as general charts, together with the special charts, thus constitute a distinct atlas of the bailiwick; and to the duplicate which is deposited in the Ducal Chamber there is appended to each special chart a copy under the name of the rough chart, which, after it has first served to calculate from it the area of each parcel, subsequently serves further to take copies of every piece of land, or of several pieces, or of the entire possessions of a man, or of each relevant passage, in order, upon payment of a fee to be rendered into the seigneurial treasury, to deliver the same to anyone who applies for it at the Ducal Chamber.

With the preparation of these special charts there is connected an inquiry into everything it may be important to know: into the name of the owner of the parcel; into the name of the parcel itself, for it almost always has its own particular name customary in the district among the inhabitants and field neighbours; into the civil character adhering to it, whether it be seigneurial, free noble land, belonging to a church or school, or subject to peasant obligations, and in the latter case, to which number of the land register it belongs; into the agricultural use made of it, whether it be garden or arable land, meadow or pasture or common; into the degree of tenure, whether it be complete private property, or subject to a co-ownership of several, or entirely waste land to which no one has yet acquired a right; into enclosure, whether it is according to the custom of the country, or whether anything special has been agreed upon or legally established in that respect; into roads and paths, whether they are open highways for general use, or ways of restricted use; into rights of way and other servitudes, whether any are fixed or otherwise established by usage; into disputes adhering to the land under neighbour law. All these particulars, together with the statement of the areal content of each parcel, are contained in an explanation of the chart accompanying each special chart. Inquiry is likewise made at every house or farmstead concerning the inhabitants, both absent and present members of the household, and concerning strangers, as well as concerning the livestock, in horses, cattle, sheep, and swine. And finally all these partial particulars are brought together into a summary, systematic abstract for each bailiwick.

At the outset the geodetic operations were undertaken according to the scale of one inch to 2,000 feet, and this was done in the bailiwick of Stuhr and the district of Neuenburg, as well as in a part of the Hausvogtei of Oldenburg; but it was afterwards found more advisable to carry out the surveys immediately according to the scale of one inch to 400 feet. For if a district or bailiwick has been taken up according to the small scale (1 inch to 2,000 feet), one cannot, in the subsequent representation on a larger scale, namely in the special charts, be content merely to take all dimensions five times as large; not everything can be expressed with the strictest exactness on the small scale, errors which on the small scale are inconspicuous and insignificant become perceptible and substantial upon a fivefold enlargement; and in particular the evaluation of areas cannot be carried out otherwise than according to the large scale, so that a renewed second measurement becomes necessary, which indeed proceeds more rapidly, since the former serves as its foundation. On the other hand, from the special charts which arise from surveys undertaken immediately according to the larger scale (1 inch to 400 feet), a general bailiwick chart can be formed, by reduction to the small scale of one inch to 2,000 feet, indoors, without therefore having to go out into the field again; and although the geodetic operations carried out directly on the larger scale advance more slowly, and we thus arrive later at the general chart of the entire duchy than could have been the case with measurement according to the smaller scale and the temporary postponement of the special charts, and although the preservation of the stakes by which the trigonometrical stations are marked is required for so much longer and demands so much greater care, yet, when the surveying business as a whole, including the detail, is considered, time and expense are nevertheless saved by this change in the initial procedure.

Since, however, according to the scale of one inch to 400 feet, not two, still less several points of the triangulation system can at the same time find room upon a single plane-table sheet, this change in procedure, with retention of the inexpressible benefit of the trigonometrical arrangements, would not have been possible, had it not been expressly the case that the plane table was found to be usable for the measurement of angles. As this is so, it is now possible for the geodetist to divide the area of land which he is to work according to the larger scale — and which, at this scale, occupies twenty-five times as much space on the paper as at the smaller — into smaller triangles, and to form series of smaller triangles, which he may continue at will, or, as he advances with his geodetic work within the district of the triangulation system, interrupt and let stand, and instead found new series of such smaller triangles upon other lines of the system of the larger ones, everywhere at his discretion and as circumstances require; and only then, when the circumstances actually arise, to fill in the larger meshes of the original triangulation net with smaller, tighter meshes.

In the surveying of the River Weser with both banks, the Hanoverian as well as our own, likewise of the coast around Butjadingen, and also of the shore of the Jade Bay, the Jeverland as well as our own, the procedure according to the smaller scale, one inch to 2,000 feet, is naturally retained. When these river and coastal charts, which are to represent the riverbed and the shore with the foreland, islands and sands or tidal flats, dikes, groynes, and buoys, as everything appears in summer at the time of the lowest ebb, shall have been completed, an examination of the depths will thereafter be undertaken, and the depths will be entered into the charts.

In the already commenced survey of the city of Oldenburg, and the preparation of a plan thereof, within the ramparts and including the same, the scale of one inch to 100 feet has been adopted, and for this likewise a trigonometrical foundation has been laid.

Fourth and Final Section.



That which every private individual who is a landowner gladly desires — namely, a chart of his possessions — naturally finds, in a pre-eminent degree and with the best of reasons, the favour of a sovereign; and faithful and instructive charts of a country are without doubt a distinguished ornament of a prince’s cabinet. Yet with a prince who is unceasingly concerned for the welfare of his land, they do not remain a mere ornament of the cabinet, but become, through the influence which they exert upon the deliberations and undertakings of such a prince, of public utility; just as, in general, useful knowledge which an enlightened and active mind acquires never remains without widely diffused benefit. In the two sovereigns who have been pleased to approve and to order the commencement and continuation of our land survey, we have found two such princes; and we can and must answer the question of the usefulness of this undertaking, in general, with confidence to the effect that these sovereigns have wished to acquire an exact local knowledge, and to place themselves in a position to survey the land, both as a whole and, as far as possible, in detail, with discerning eyes from their cabinet. I say: with confidence and with modesty we must ourselves give this answer, and thus remove all anxious apprehension opposed to these two dispositions, as though this undertaking could have any intentions other than beneficent ones. Just as little as this undertaking, in its execution, has been a burden upon the land in general — not even through any unpaid service — just as little will it, in its consequences, be to the detriment of any landowner. With the same confidence and modesty we must also leave to such a sovereign authority the application of the knowledge which it endeavours to acquire, and not be hasty in ascribing this or that particular intention; it will, according to time and circumstances, make wise use thereof, and it does not follow that it must necessarily have immediate aims at the present moment.

Meanwhile, manifold and great benefits lie plainly before our eyes, which I shall now proceed to explain.

A great part of the duchy consists of waste, uncultivated land, and it will assuredly — just as it is of the highest interest to curiosity — be in itself of importance to determine with precision in what proportion the land not yet brought under cultivation stands to that which is already cultivated, both in the duchy as a whole and in the individual bailiwicks and districts in particular. In the strict sense no soil is absolutely unusable, and there are cultivated districts near great cities which by nature are in no respect better than our Osenberge; yet of a considerable degree of cultivation in such districts — which is possible only under a most rare combination of favourable circumstances — one cannot here think. Nor do we possess tracts of land fertile in any marked degree which have not already been taken up; however sparse the population may be in certain parts of the duchy, it is nowhere so sparse that there should be room for colonists — people who would live solely by agriculture and to whom so much usable land could be assigned that they might subsist upon it alone. The further cultivation of the land is therefore to be expected only from such persons as possess another means of livelihood, alongside which they at the same time carry on the cultivation of a parcel of land to be rendered productive; thus scarcely from foreigners, but only from natives, and only gradually.

Now it is indeed in itself a good thing that many a piece of land from the waste tracts has been assigned for cultivation and has actually become productive, even though from time to time one or another has not become so, or has not remained so, but has again fallen into decay, and that more is assigned daily; yet it is unquestionably better if the use of the still uncultivated land proceeds according to a systematic plan, if in doing so regard is had to the old inhabitants and their situation along the waste tracts, since the new cultivators to be expected from the bosom of the old families are assuredly the most reliable; if one cannot always at will select the better or the less bad from among what is altogether bad, until finally nothing remains but that utterly bad which has meanwhile been left lying and with which nothing further can be done; if, through arbitrarily assigned and occupied patches, an irregular fragmentation is not at last produced, which everywhere makes a systematic distribution — such as one might later wish to undertake — impossible and binds one’s hands on all sides. Nor can it be maintained that if now an entire large tract of uncultivated land were at once, instead of being gradually assigned, distributed among the neighbouring old inhabitants, thereby at the same time a limit and obstacle would be set to the further growth of the population; rather, the only change resulting for those who later seek land would be that they would have to apply for it no longer to the sovereign authority, but to the owners of the distributed territory, as many a cottager and smallholder has done and still does.

Furthermore, there still prevails in this country much communal tenure of land — communities of various kinds: with actual joint ownership of soil and ground; with claims to certain uses without co-ownership of the soil; among the subjects among themselves, and between them and the sovereign — commons, rights of pasture and grazing, rights and drift-ways. It is acknowledged, and among land experts throughout the wider world no longer a matter of dispute, that communal landholding is harmful and that its abolition is advisable: what is enjoyed by all is properly used by none, tended by none, nor duly maintained; improvements are not thought of, and if one person thinks of them, he does not prevail. There are certainly considerable stretches of grassland from which meadows might be made, which now serve only as pasture; it is certain that the grazing of livestock in forests is destructive to young growth and regeneration, both in deciduous woods and in coniferous forests; that of the cultivation of coniferous timber — the only thing to which certain regions are anywhere still suited, the only means by which they can yet be assisted — there can be no thought at all if livestock are not kept out; it is certain that the arbitrary and unsupervised burning of heath is harmful in more than one respect, and so forth.

When the described detailed maps are available, it then becomes possible to survey where distributions of the uncultivated land not yet taken up, not yet assigned, and where abolitions of communal ownership are to be undertaken — to be ordered, imposed, promoted, and encouraged; it becomes possible to survey and assess how each such operation, according to the situation of the land which can be detached in connection with the dwellings and holdings of the neighbouring inhabitants, may most advantageously be approached, and a plan for it drawn up with prior mature deliberation in the cabinet, before proceeding to practical action in the field; it becomes possible for the sovereign to assess what might perhaps sooner or later be undertaken with those stretches of land which will still remain after every distribution judged advisable — for the sovereign, in whose eyes land, even when according to the proverbial rhyme it is literally nothing but sand, nevertheless retains a not inconsiderable value solely in consideration of the fact that it is always a part of the German Imperial soil allotted to him.

The accurate representation of the cultivated land that constitutes the property of the settled inhabitants leads to an improved description of the country, in which everything now becomes visible to the eye. The benevolent sovereign need not be animated by selfish motives in order to find pleasure and interest in contemplating the sources of his subjects’ prosperity, and it may readily be foreseen how such reflections can guide him in his efforts and measures for the protection, preservation, purification, and expansion of those sources.

This representation serves to elucidate the tax registers or land books. One learns the extent of each individual parcel; one knows of which parcels each consolidated farmstead, large or small, under its various designations, is composed; one knows, when permission is sought for the alienation or separation of one or another parcel, its relation to the whole, in order to be able to judge what is expedient. One also comes to know precisely the land exempt from the taxes resting on peasant holdings — land belonging to the lordship, the nobility, churches, schools, and foundations. It then becomes easy to record all changes occurring in subsequent times, since the present condition has once been established, and each sheet or small sheet intended for such additions can be given its clear and concise reference to the representation of the current status. Mutual understanding becomes easy and free of all ambiguity, and the collegiate body, the official, and the subject have at hand a ready common means of comprehension.

This representation also assists landowners by providing them with situation maps of their possessions, which they no longer need to have surveyed themselves, but need only redeem as a copy. Nothing secures the local basis of property more firmly than drawings taken from a public collection that carries full credibility in itself.

And finally, such maps — so precise and so thoroughly detailed — even serve the cause of justice in all cases where the decision of disputes depends upon locality. With respect to accuracy and impartiality they deserve unquestionable credibility, for the surveyor reliably sets down on paper what he has before him in nature. Future disputes are forestalled, since once the present status has been established by the map, no alterations can occur without being immediately demonstrable; and existing disputes, which likewise — as stated — are noted insofar as they come to light, may perhaps be resolved amicably through the parties’ own reflection, or else, in order to remove them altogether, be taken up ex officio by the authorities, examined, and decided.

G. C. Oeder.
