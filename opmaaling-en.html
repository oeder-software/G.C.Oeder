Method for the Determination of the Area of the Lands.


When, at the beginning of the year 1770, I received the commission to compile extracts from the lists which had arrived in accordance with the census of the people in the royal Danish dominions in Europe, decreed for the 15th of August 1769, to draw conclusions therefrom, and to accompany all with my annotations, I was greatly concerned with determining the area of these lands; for it is of little use to know how many inhabitants a country has, if one does not also know how large it is.

Already earlier, at the beginning of the year 1769, reflections on the consequences of liberty and property within the peasant estate had prompted me to consider a convenient method for determining the geographical extent of a country, or its area; for, as I desired, so I also hoped to be able, through clear calculations, to demonstrate that—under otherwise equal circumstances—the population, as well as every form of general prosperity, is greatest where the peasant estate, by far the most numerous class among the citizens of the state, enjoys the blessings of civil liberty in the greatest measure. In the hope of gradually obtaining more and more information regarding the number of inhabitants—which, unexpectedly to me, was soon fulfilled through the census decreed in that same year—I began in the meantime with the determination of the area, and had cause, upon receiving the aforementioned commission, to be pleased that I had already prepared this part of the work in advance.

Once, while I was engaged in determining the area and happened to have before me a balance scale (of the kind described and illustrated in Gellert’s Metallurgy), it occurred to me that such a scale might best serve my purpose. On so sensitive a balance, I thought, even small parcels—especially of map paper—should be perceptible; and if one might assume that the paper, throughout the entire sheet, save perhaps the margin, possessed a uniform thickness, then I had found what I was seeking. For two countries depicted upon the same sheet would then relate to each other in spatial extent as the weights of the cut-out pieces of the sheet on which they are drawn. The expense of a single copy of a map would be a trifle in comparison with the great convenience this method would afford for determining the size of a country and its parts. Such a method must indeed be convenient, for countries—especially those situated by the sea—commonly possess not only an irregular outline and border, but their political and civil division into provinces and districts likewise brings with it similar irregularities of contour. Were one to attempt, in measuring the area, to follow all those straight and curved bays, corners, and points, such a measurement would be as intolerable as it would, in truth, be unreliable. By weighing, the measurement remains equally simple, whatever the shape of the country, and equally reliable, provided one assumes the paper to be of uniform thickness—which may readily be ascertained by cutting the sheet into equally sized squares and weighing these against one another.

Every map has its scale, according to which one may predefine a square of arbitrary size as the modulus, and every map possesses, in one or more of its four corners, an empty space not belonging to the actual land represented, where the modulus may be inscribed — which one may choose at will, though it is better to choose it larger rather than smaller. And even if no such unused space were present, it would matter little; for once the modulus has been weighed, one may also cut out and weigh separately any portion that might belong to the land being measured and that was included in the modulus. Thus, one cuts out the modulus and every part of the land to be measured carefully from the sheet (from one and the same sheet, to be sure), weighs all these pieces separately with care, and then applies the regula de tri: the modulus, a piece of paper which weighs thus much, represents so many square miles — therefore, another piece of paper weighing so and so much must represent so many miles. — And this is the method, which is in itself as simple as Columbus’ trick with the egg, and yet enables us — in a phrase which sounds rather poetic but is literally true — to weigh countries!

It goes without saying that this method cannot be properly applied to planiglobes, nor to any representation of a considerably large portion of the globe — I speak not merely of the Russian or Turkish empires, but even of any moderately extensive European kingdom; thus, it is not suitable for general maps. Should one nonetheless attempt to apply it, the resulting area will invariably appear larger than that which is obtained afterward by summing the results from the weighing of the more detailed, special maps.

It is equally self-evident that this measurement presupposes accurate maps; for if they be not so, no other method of measurement is of much greater use.

It is to be wished that all those who undertake such measurements by weighing would make their results publicly known, thereby sparing others — who may lack the same opportunity — the cost of sacrificing maps for the purpose. The map employed should always be named, and impressions used for weighing ought, as far as possible, to be printed on good, strong, and not overly thin paper.

He who is disinclined to busy himself with the mechanical part of the operation may content himself with delineating the modulus and then proceeding with the calculation, while leaving the cutting and weighing to others — provided only that all is carried out with due care.

I do not know what method Templeman — whose statements concerning the size and population of countries may be read abundantly in books and almanacs — may have employed for determining area, for I have not seen his book, entitled Survey of the Globe.

As more and more such measurements of countries, and more and more reports concerning the population of lands, become known, it is to be wished and hoped that these scattered notices may from time to time be gathered together, and that in such collections — with accompanying calculations of how many people fall to each square mile (geometrical square mile) — one might at a glance survey the population of each country. Such a compilation would form one of the firmest foundations for statistical reasoning; for only upon the basis of such determined proportions of population can one begin to inquire into the causes of the differences in population, and these causes may be sought in the varying quality of the soil, the productivity of land and water, the climate, the geographical position, the trades and occupations, the industry of the inhabitants, the domestic political constitution of the countries, the spirit of the times, and so forth.

I herewith present, for the present occasion, the determinations of area already undertaken in the year 1769 for the Kingdom of Denmark, for the Duchies of Schleswig and Holstein, and for the Counties of Oldenburg and Delmenhorst, which at that time were under Danish sovereignty.

For Denmark, I employed the map from Pontoppidan’s Atlas Daniae; for the Copenhagen district, the Wessel maps; for Schleswig and Holstein, the maps of Dankwerth; and for Oldenburg and Delmenhorst, the map by Hunrich.
