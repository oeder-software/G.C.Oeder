Thoughts on how the French debts could have been paid off without paper money.
On Hammond, a banknote manufacturer in Copenhagen 2c.

Extract of a letter from Oldenburg, dated 18 December 1790.

… I am astonished how the National Assembly could have missed the right path, which lies so near at hand. It ought to have done—what indeed has been done in part—namely, to draw up the statement of the debts; but thereafter it should have divided this mass of debt into equal portions of 10,000 livres, and numbered them in a continuous numerical series; which, assuming the total debt at two milliards, would yield 200,000 portions and as many numbers.

Further, for each creditor or participant in the mass of debt, a bond should have been issued, in which he would be acknowledged, in the name of the nation, as creditor for such and such an amount; so and so many portions would be assigned to him, and their numbers specified in the bond, together with the promise that he should, in due order, arrive at payment, meanwhile enjoy annual interest thereon, and also possess the power to cede his bond, with designation of the cessionary.

Thereupon a division fund should be established, entirely separate from the ordinary finances, that is, from the administration of the usual annual revenues and expenditures. This arrangement should be made in such a manner that a surplus of revenue over expenditure would remain; and this surplus would flow into the redemption fund. Into this same redemption fund would also flow, wholly and entirely, the proceeds from the sale of the national properties, to be paid in hard, ringing coin.

As soon as any considerable sum (say one million livres), beyond what is required for the payment of interest, had accumulated in the redemption fund, a dividend of 100 portions would be drawn, by lot, par tirage, so that those creditors would arrive at payment whose bond numbers were found among those drawn. There would thus remain 1,900 millions in bonds outstanding, and from the redemption fund one million livres in hard, ringing coin would be brought into circulation.

No paper endowed with the nature of money would be instituted at all; and as soon as the competition between paper money and effective coin ceased, one would see and experience that the latter—or metal fit for coinage—would emerge within the country from chests and coffers; that French money driven beyond the borders of the realm would return; and that from wealthy cultivators abroad, for example from both the United and the Belgian Netherlands, it would be brought into France.

Of all this there can be no thought so long as the competition between paper money and ringing coin endures; rather, the latter is unavoidably driven out by the former. — Yet there are also other states in which one refuses to see what everything ultimately depends upon, namely, that the contracted debt must be deprived of the nature of money: see above, Fascicle 59, p. 298, §19, and p. 304, §32.

To the same place, p. 294, concerning Hammond.

It will now be about thirty years since he was beheaded in Copenhagen, on account of the fabrication of false banco notes, together with his accomplice Sumblad, a Swede. They confined themselves solely to notes of 100 rix-dollars; these were, omnium consensu, astonishingly similar, and it is quite possible that some of them may even now still be in circulation.

Hammond was a Norman, of peasant origin as far as I know; a clear-headed man, of handsome stature and appearance, and of a polished manner of life. His skill and other good qualities gained him the favour of a major-general, a man of understanding and reputation, under whose regiment—one of the Trondheim national regiments—he served for a time, as he also did in Copenhagen with the Foot Guards; thereafter, however, he entered the civil service, and was a Councillor of Chancery when he fell under inquisition.

The trade was discovered by chance, when in Bergen, in the hands of an attentive man—the diocesan treasury clerk, later diocesan governor, Petersen—there were found two notes bearing one and the same number. This chance occurrence was brought about by the circumstance that the royal monies flowing into the diocesan treasury often lay there for some time awaiting the government’s disposition, and Mr. Petersen conceived the idea of arranging the notes remaining with him according to their numerical sequence; to which he was further prompted by the fact that banco notes had of late begun to become more frequent in Norway than before.

Thus one traced the course by which the notes had made their way to Bergen, and was led to the region near Friedrichshald and Friedrichsstadt, where Hammond resided. To this was added Hammond’s own imprudence, namely that he could not deny himself the enjoyment of his prosperity, and maintained a suspicious expenditure, which was not concealed by the various projects with which he sought to mask his true occupation—projects that, to his misfortune, led him to Copenhagen.

Hammond’s share in the fabrication consisted in the imitation of the handwriting; Sumblad’s share lay in the engraving and the stamp. The most difficult part for them—because it is hardest to keep secret—the manufacture of the paper, was carried out in Hammond’s cellar, from which they knew how to remove his otherwise entirely innocent wife by pretending to practise alchemy.

These people found themselves far more in ecclesia pressa than those virtuosi in Germany, Switzerland, Italy, the Netherlands, and England, against whom the National Assembly is warned by Mr. Bergasse.

You will now readily feel the force of what I say in §10 concerning a clever fraudster, and especially one who is cautious in the enjoyment of his acquired prosperity—that such a man easily evades chance. It was this imprudence, not lack of skill, that led these neat fellows to ruin.

It is well known that the notes have a border of foliage ornament, of which something remains when they are cut out of the sheet on which they are printed; and when the notes are numbered, the number which a cut-out note carries into the public is written down at the place where it had been situated. One can therefore fit a suspicious note, in the bank, into the sheet from which it is supposed to have been cut. But this is no means of discovery unless there is already other suspicion.

English notes are held to be harder to imitate than others; but the forger experiments with the mixture of materials for the paper until he finds the right one. And, in short, what human hands make, other human hands can imitate.

von Oeder.
