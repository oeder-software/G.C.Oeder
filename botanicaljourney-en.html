Report of My Foreign Journey in the Year 1754-55.
Most Humble Submission,
and Sent from Schleswig,
on the 2nd of April 1755.
Georg Christian Oeder.

I will divide the report of my journey, undertaken by order of His Royal Majesty, through Holland to England and France, and through Germany to inspect foreign botanical institutions and to compare them with the plan for the botanical institution intended by His Majesty, which I had the honor of most humbly presenting before my departure, into two parts.

In the first part, I will provide information about the state of botany among foreign nations, as I have been able to observe.

In the second part, I will present what I, after the said comparison and continued deeper reflection, deem beneficial for the greater perfection of my mentioned plan, and thus for the Royal Institution itself. To this, I append an annex of opinions expressed in writing by some knowledgeable men regarding my plan, in the original as well as in copies and translations.

My thoughts regarding the arrangement and construction of the enclosing wall, the greenhouses, and the overall local layout of the garden will be presented separately after a further inspection of the site and a conversation with the Royal Court Architect, General Major von Thurah. I therefore most humbly request the necessary postponement and corresponding instruction for arranging such a conversation.

FIRST PART - On Holland

In Holland, I have visited the academic gardens in Utrecht and Leyden, as well as the medicinal gardens in Amsterdam and The Hague.

The garden in Utrecht is newly established. It is too small, but otherwise well stocked. The current professor, Mr. Wachendorff, a wealthy man, contributes a lot from his own fortune. For what the curators of the university have provided, according to Mr. Wachendorff's report, is by no means sufficient. Thus, the garden will deteriorate under another professor who neither can nor will spend what Mr. Wachendorff does.

The garden in Leyden is one of the oldest academic gardens and has been one of the most remarkable since its founding, and still is, although my impression formed from Mr. von Royen's Flora Leydensis has been somewhat diminished. It is maintained, like the whole university, jointly by the provinces of Holland, West Friesland, and Zeeland. By their order, plants must be supplied from all Dutch colonies, making it easy to acquire African plants and those from both Indies. However, the greenhouses seem too small to me, and the gardener, with two helpers for all the work, too few. The academic holidays, which fall in summer and last for six weeks, are also observed in the garden. Last year, when Mr. Professor von Royen

handed over his botanical professorship to his nephew, it was transferred from the medical faculty to the philosophical one, yet the garden is still nothing more than a medicinal garden. In Leyden, I met a young man whom the Academy of Sciences in Saint Petersburg has sent on travels for several years to train as a botanist.

The medicinal garden in Amsterdam is the best maintained. It is spacious and equipped with very beautiful, large greenhouses, and the gardener is provided with eight assistants every day. Among the directors, there are always some from the large merchants, and currently, one of them is Mr. Cliffort, from whose own garden Mr. Linnaeus wrote the famous book Hortus Cliffortianus, and the garden naturally does not lack plants from the Dutch colonies. A large part of the expenses comes from a fee that all doctors, surgeons, apothecaries, and their apprentices and journeymen are obliged to pay, and the other part is provided by the magistrate. The total expenses amount to about 3000 rixdollars. Since the time of the Commelins, there has been a small library and some volumes of paintings, but neither is clearly defined, which means they remain fragmentary.

I was also surprised that in the many hours I spent in the garden during my stay in Amsterdam, I did not see anyone there seeking to learn, and thus so many plants bloom only for the gardener. Mr. Burmann, professor of botany, also complained about the lack of enthusiasts. The gardener, with the prior knowledge of the curators, can sell certain plants, of which there is a supply, on their account, and when the Royal Garden can be stocked, it may be wise to acquire plants from the Dutch colonies there, and, according to Mr. Tronchin’s advice, also request the magistrate to provide assistance in this matter. For the medicinal garden in The Hague, Mr. Professor Schwencke began the work last year, and it was underway that the magistrate should buy the garden he had prepared for this purpose and establish an institution for the benefit of the local surgeons and apothecaries.

A general remark I would make about all these gardens is that it seems to me the botanists there hold too little correspondence, being content with what is brought to them from their nation's colonies, and that they lack plants from the large European, especially the eastern, continent. There is also no provision for botanists to correspond without incurring losses.

I also do not like that gardeners are allowed to charge money for access to these gardens, and that this income is considered part of their salary. It is burdensome for many learners and true enthusiasts, and for the gardener and his people, it is a waste of time when they, for their own benefit, must deal with all the ignorant spectators.

In economic botany, the Dutch skill and diligence are undeniably worthy of emulation. They make the most of their land, of which each has, of course, only a small portion. All of Holland looks like a garden. Necessity and the desire for profit have made them skillful. The efforts of a wise king to encourage his subjects and make their industry more fruitful through the spread of greater knowledge is a major reason for the promotion of the gardener's art. Most of what applies in Holland must also apply in Denmark. The winter is not much harsher here, and the atmospheric conditions are mostly the same.

On England

In England, public institutions have not contributed much to the advancement of botany, but the wealth of the inhabitants, combined with the natural charm and appeal of country life, has made botany more widespread in this country than in any other. Just as most travelers prefer the variety in the English landscapes over the uniformity in Holland, the English taste in gardening also deserves preference over the Dutch. The eye is not restricted to the small art of dry symmetry in paths, hedges, and parterres, but is instead allowed a free view of nature, which pleases in its grandeur, making one feel as though they are in the finest part of a beautiful region. One is not wearied by the colorful florist’s game of insignificant variations of a few flower species, but rather sees truly different plants that seem to have been produced by nature itself in their locations.

The English do not highly value the plants in their gardens that must be forced in greenhouses and by artificial methods, but rather prefer those that want to grow freely in the open land and air. Their North American colonies provide them with a great quantity of these, which they gradually try to naturalize in England. They especially love trees and shrubs, from which they create small forests around their country houses. One can judge how widespread this taste has already become from the fact that several gardeners around London sustain themselves well from large plantations, selling hundreds of such open-grown plants, which one seldom sees individually in gardens specifically designed for that purpose.

I have seen two public botanical gardens. The garden of the Apothecaries' Society in London, located in Chelsea, was expanded by Hans Sloane through his generosity, but otherwise, I found nothing particularly remarkable about this institution. The current advantages of the garden are more due to the special diligence and skill of the experienced gardener Mr. Philip Miller than to the original design of the institution.

The garden in Oxford was founded by a private person in the previous century, and in this century, Jacob Sherard, an apothecary in London, has improved this foundation. The layout of the garden and the house where the professor resides, as well as the herbaria belonging to Morison, Bobart, Sherard, and Dillenius, are quite beautiful and impressive, but the annual funding does not seem very substantial. The professor has revenues of 3000 pounds, the gardener has 20 pounds for correspondence and plant purchases, and all incidental expenses fall on the curators of the Sherard Foundation each time.

This Sherard garden actually does not belong to the University of Oxford, but one can also recognize the old scholastic structure of this university by the fact that, according to their constitution, very little has been done for botany, chemistry, or anatomy, or other sciences beyond the speculative ones.

In the extensive American colonies, many doctors have settled, among whom there are always some herbalists who provide more knowledge about the local plants and the plants themselves. In the last year, a physician I know returned to England after a nine-year stay in Jamaica, Barbados, and Antigua, which share the climate and conditions of the Caribbean islands, and has noted much more and better than Hans Sloane. Mr. Collinson, about whom there is an addition in the appendix, has a correspondent in Pennsylvania (Collinson himself is a Quaker) whose profession is to collect seeds from the local plants for enthusiasts in Europe, which will mostly thrive outdoors in Denmark, as I have seen in Mr. Collinson's garden, or as it was with the Virginian plants at Mr. Gronov's in Leiden.

I would like to briefly mention a small journey I took to the coast of Kent with a learned merchant, Mr. Ellis, to conduct some investigations on sea products, which have until recently been regarded as plants. Our observations have confirmed that these disputed bodies are animal products and habitats of animals. Mr. Ellis will now likely be finished with his book that he intended to write on this subject. On my part, I will keep these matters in mind while in Norway.

On France.

The Royal Botanical Garden in Paris is, according to its original foundation, also known as a garden for medicinal plants. It was established under Louis XIII, and Gui de la Brosse, who made the initial proposal for it and became its first professor, had his memoirs printed for the king, Richelieu, and other ministers, along with the king's patent. Under Louis XIV, professorships in chemistry and anatomy were also created for the garden, as the institutions in the old Parisian university, just as I have mentioned regarding the English universities, are quite poor in the medical faculty. However, all these professors have their appointments issued as professors of botany. The total expenditure for the current institution is no more than 20,000 livres, of which the Intendant du Jardin du Roy receives 6,000, the first professor of botany 1,500, and the second 1,200, while the gardener gets 2,800, which he must use to pay the wages of all the workers. A draftsman is also employed, and currently, it is a women’s room. In the garden, there is also the Cabinet du Roy, along with a guard and demonstrator. The space is larger than necessary and is merely a burden for the gardener. The greenhouses are old and spacious. There is no library associated with the garden, but there are large collections of dried herbs, especially from Tournefort's Levantine journey, and in the Royal Library, there is a collection of 60 volumes of painted plants that has no equal, which was already initiated in the early part of the last century by Gaston d'Orleans.

For the past four or five years, the King of France has had a botanical garden established in Trianon for his personal pleasure, which he frequently visits to view the plants. The greenhouse is the finest of its kind that I have seen. The gardener has orders to procure everything possible and have it brought in from other countries, whatever can be sourced. Similarly, the Duc d'Ayen, eldest son of the Duc de Noailles, has established a similar garden in St. Germain, his governorship, around the same time for his pleasure. This garden deserves precedence over that of Trianon in several respects, particularly the arboretum, which contains everything that can be considered beautiful in this regard and provides a captivating view with many foreign trees growing in the open air.

In this garden, the skilled physician Mr. le Monnier, a practitioner and hospital doctor in St. Germain, provides valuable service to the Duke. Furthermore, near the park in Courbevoie, Mr. de Bombarde has a similar garden and is particularly focused on the cultivation of foreign plants. Mr. du Hamel, Inspecteur General de la Marine, is also a lover of botany and a practical botanist.

Since the French also have large colonies in America, and for a long time there have been great botanists in France, with the two brothers, Msr. de Jussieu, having grown old in the field of botany and having had the opportunity to train many good students, who are now spread across various parts of France and also in America, and since the transportation of plants is promoted as best as possible by the government, with all the aforementioned gardens supporting one another, and each enthusiast always receiving something special from their correspondents, it is clear that these gardens collectively contain a larger number of different plants than in any other country. Every year, a significant number of new plants arrive from America, which had not previously been seen in Europe; for example, the brother of the aforementioned Msr. de Jussieu sends many plants from Peru from time to time. From Senegal, a student of Bernard de Jussieu, after a four-year stay, has brought various plants in good seeds, as well as valuable information from this little-explored part of Africa. Canada supplies Siberian and Norwegian plants, as clear evidence that the conclusion regarding common plants between countries is generally well-founded.

Now, Mr. du Hamel's book on trees and shrubs that can be grown in the open in France is likely published. Mr. du Hamel plants on his estates, based on the information he receives from his correspondents along with the plants themselves from America, each on the spots where the soil and location are most favorable, thereby turning his entire estate into a garden. Mr. du Hamel is well-known as a practical botanist and insightful landowner from various memoirs, particularly from Traité sur l'agriculture. I wish Denmark had many such men. France would have more of them if the establishment at Jardin du Roy were expanded, and if such encouragement inspired more people like Mr. du Hamel, who act out of passion and personal initiative.

On Germany

I have visited three gardens in Germany. In the Margrave's garden in Karlsruhe, one can still see the remnants of the large collection of foreign plants that the previous Margrave had gathered. The current Margrave does not continue this project on the same scale, likely because it exceeds his financial means. However, the most beautiful orangerie, possibly the finest outside of Italy, still remains there.

The Göttingen garden, where I studied botany under Mr. von Haller, owes its supply of plants that can be grown from seeds to Mr. von Haller’s extensive correspondence. However, it has always been lacking in shrubs. There were shortcomings in its initial setup, and with the minimal resources allocated to it, it is bound to deteriorate.

The Helmstedt garden is regarded as one of the most significant in Germany. Mr. von Sprekelsen of Hamburg enriched it with the collection of foreign plants from his own garden. However, the greenhouses appeared faulty to me, and overall, I noticed nothing particularly remarkable about the establishment.

All academic gardens in Germany are, as such, solely dedicated to a single purpose within botany, serving merely as medicinal gardens. As such, they do not justify or reward a large investment, and in general, the resources allocated to the German academies are not generous. Additionally, overland transportation makes it difficult and costly to assemble a substantial collection of foreign plants. From these circumstances, it becomes clear that one should not expect to find well-supplied botanical gardens in Germany. What wealthy enthusiasts do here and there is incidental and not of great significance to botany overall.

At the otherwise renowned University of Halle, there has so far been no academic garden; however, I have recently heard that the King of Prussia intends to establish one there.

I previously visited the botanical garden in Berlin during my stay there. It is unnecessarily large, and since it is far from the city, it cannot be visited without considerable time loss. I am not aware that it is used for anything other than medicinal purposes, even though it is under the Academy of Sciences. In Stuttgart lives a man named Martini, who accompanied Mr. Gmelin in Siberia and now cultivates most of the plants brought from there in Stuttgart. Since Mr. Gmelin, due to illness, is unable to continue working, it is to be feared that Flora Sibirica, the fruit of a nine-year journey, will not be completed. The aforementioned Martini still has duplicates of dried herbs, and from these, he could form a herbarium worthy of being preserved in a public botanical library, so that the memory of these Siberian plants does not completely fade.

Now I will conclude the first part of my most humble report with the following remarks.

All these botanical institutions in the various countries I have mentioned are based on the notions that have only recently emerged about botany in the world, according to which it is regarded as part of the medical science. Thus, it has almost exclusively been doctors who have pursued botany, and those physicians engaged with patients have left botany to those removed from the bedside and the opportunity for practical experience. As a result, far from everything that could be done with this narrow view of botany has been accomplished. Furthermore, it is far from proven that plants exist solely to serve humans directly, especially as medicinal remedies. Therefore, it is impossible for a medical botanist to give a satisfactory answer to the frequent question: What is the use of knowing 6,000 herbs if one only knows how to utilize at most 500 of them? Thus, the emptiness of botany under this conception is revealed. Consequently, botany has been viewed as a relatively useless science by those who promote the sciences, and botanists have been considered somewhat impractical individuals. The economic side of botany has been left to empirical practitioners, who proceed without scientific knowledge or reasoning, treating it as a mere craft.

With such incomplete concepts, all botanical institutions have also remained incomplete, and it is easier to establish entirely new institutions than to reform an already existing, albeit outdated, system. A serious issue with these older institutions is that even if the original founders provided adequately for the preservation of the foundation according to their ideas, any institution based on cash funds will lose value over time due to the changing value of precious metals.
