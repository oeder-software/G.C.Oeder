A German geographical mile contains 23,626 Rhenish feet.

Of these, 15 make one degree of the equator.

According to the late Councillor of State Hunrichs, 11½ Oldenburg miles correspond to one degree of the equator. Thus 3 Oldenburg miles are equal to 4 geographical miles, and one Oldenburg mile contains 31,501⅓ Rhenish feet.

On this occasion I explain and correct the heading of the above-mentioned table on page 483 of the first volume of these sheets. The lines to which the distances listed in that table are referred are namely not exactly the meridian and perpendicular of Oldenburg, and the matter stands as follows.

When the first trigonometric chart was to be drawn, no meridian had yet been determined, and therefore two lines were drawn from the observatory by compass: one approximately running south and north, the other perpendicular to it. From these two lines the distances of the objects were calculated. Later, when the observatory’s meridian had been determined, it was found that the northern end of the compass meridian deviated from the true meridian by 17 minutes 15.5 seconds toward the west, and thus corresponded to a point whose distance eastward from the compass meridian is 76,886 feet, which point happened to coincide with the church tower of Bardewisch.

For this reason Mr. Wessel has called the compass meridian a parallel to the Bardewisch meridian. However one may name this line, it is to be noted that its northern end deviates from the northern end of the Oldenburg meridian by 17 minutes 15.5 or 16 seconds toward the west.

Since there was no necessity to compute the distances of the objects precisely from the meridian and its perpendicular, but their position could be referred to any two lines whatsoever, provided only that the angle of these with the meridian was determined, Mr. Wessel, in order to avoid reworking the earlier computations, remained, even after the true meridian had been determined, with calculating the distances from the compass meridian and its perpendicular, but at the same time, in calculating the longitude and latitude of the objects, always took into account the angle which the assumed meridian makes with the true one.

For if one were to regard the two lines to which the objects are referred as the true meridian and perpendicular of Oldenburg, one would make the longitude and latitude of the objects a few seconds too large or too small.

On page 484 there is to be added:
