Conference Councillor Hübner’s and Finance Councillor Oeder’s Draft for a Union of the Charlottenborg Natural Cabinet with the University.
(See this year’s 2nd issue No. XI.)




“His Royal Majesty has most graciously been pleased, on the 15th of last March, to command us the undersigned to assemble and make the necessary arrangements for a union of the Charlottenborg Institute with the University, and moreover to take into consideration the following points:

1)That Professors Brünniche and Fabritius be appointed as lecturers.
2)That the maintenance fund be wholly taken from the revenues of the University, if possible immediately, or at least in the course of time; meanwhile the deficiency shall be assigned from the Private Treasury, once the amount has previously been determined.
3)That we should draw up a plan in what manner this Institute may become most useful to the Public.”

“In virtue of this most gracious command we have forthwith obtained from various departments, and from the University itself, the necessary information and explanations; we have thereupon instituted certain local inquiries and inspections; and are at length, after mature deliberation upon the entire scope of the matter, induced to submit the following most humble report.”

1.



“With respect to the appointment of a Professor of historia naturalis and a Professor of oeconomia as lecturers at the University in the philosophical Faculty, there appears to be no objection. It is true that among the seven chairs in this Faculty, which the Foundation of the University, as its fundamental law, establishes, none was originally destined for these sciences. Yet since the manner of study, which since the publication of that law—otherwise containing exceedingly much that is good—has become far more practical, has already assumed a different aspect, and will doubtless assume it still further to the advantage of the people; we can scarcely deem this circumstance a difficulty; the more so as, as we shall hereafter show, room may by certain alterations be made for these sciences within the said Faculty, without its being for the present necessary to increase the number of professorial chairs.”

2.



“The expenses necessary for the maintenance of this Institute, thus united with the University, concern:

a) The first requisite establishments.
b) The maintenance and enlargement of these establishments.
c) The salaries of the two Professors attached thereto.

For the first establishments there might be applied the portion, fallen due from July 1769 to the end of September 1770, of the 1000 Rix-dollars West Indian Courant, which His Majesty has enjoined the Town Bailiff at Christianstad in St. Croix annually to pay out of his official revenues, and concerning which His Majesty has not yet disposed.

The said 1000 Rix-dollars West Indian Courant, amounting to 750 Rix-dollars Danish Courant, and, as above mentioned, payable from the 1st of July 1769, were by a Rescript of the 15th of June 1770 most graciously bestowed by His Majesty upon the University, and their application at the same time determined, excepting 100 Rix-dollars and the amount of five quarters of a year, reckoned from the 1st of July 1769 to the last of September 1770, concerning which His Majesty has reserved to Himself further disposition.

This amount constitutes 1250 Rix-dollars West Indian, or 937 Rix-dollars 3 Marks Danish Courant; which sum we consider sufficient for the most necessary initial arrangements.*)

*) How this Royal donation is properly to be understood, I shall endeavour to elucidate at the conclusion of this Report.“

“For the annual maintenance of these manifestly beneficial institutions, we at present perceive no possibility of discovering more than 200 Rix-dollars; namely, 100 Rix-dollars which the aforementioned most gracious Rescript has already assigned to the University’s Cabinet of Natural History, and the remaining annual 100 Rix-dollars of the above-mentioned sum, not yet disposed of, which might be allotted to the Professor of Oeconomy for the procurement of the requisite models and other related expenses.

In order likewise not to neglect, from the very beginning, the utility of the chemical laboratory, it might be recommended to the Professor of Natural History to employ the first 1000 Rix-dollars annually designated for the purchase of specimens for the University, and thereafter likewise to be so employed, in the first instance for chemical experiments in the course of his lectures. This may the more easily and economically be done, as the University acquires the considerable advantage of obtaining at once the entire Royal Cabinet of Natural History, and therefore has no need laboriously to assemble from the outset a collection of this kind—toward which, in fact, the said 100 Rix-dollars were solely and exclusively intended—without thereby for a long time having been able to procure anything sufficiently serviceable.”

“Moreover, we find ourselves hereby moved, in all dutiful submission, to represent: that the often-mentioned West Indian monies, as well by reason of the remoteness of the place as by the difficulty attending their collection, cannot constitute any secure fund for the University; unless it should please the King Himself to undertake their receipt, and instead to assign to the University an equivalent payable with regularity from a Royal Treasury here. The most proper course might perhaps be, that the Finance Chamber receive orders for this collection, and that the payment to the University be made from the Royal Exchequer. Otherwise, we have reason to foresee that the failure of the whole or of a part of these subsidies would occasion a stagnation detrimental to the advancement of the sciences, and render these noble institutions useless, or perhaps even reduce them altogether to nothing.”

“In order to regulate the salaries of the two Professors with some advantage to the Royal Treasury, we perceive but one expedient. We propose, namely, that they retain the same annual salary which they at present enjoy as an interim provision, until, according to the prospect which shall shortly be set forth, they may, by virtue of their seniority, ascend within the philosophical Faculty and attain to actual and permanent revenues.”

“The first of these interim salaries, amounting to 300 Rix-dollars for Prof. Brünniche, might be assigned to him in such manner that the revenues of the choir establishment at Our Lady’s Church contribute thereto annually 200 Rix-dollars, and the revenues of the University Library 100 Rix-dollars.

The said choir establishment is indeed the property of the aforesaid Church. Yet as this Church itself belongs to the University, and the choir establishment may for the present well bear this charge, we do not consider such an assignment inequitable. As to the Library, it is admittedly not wealthy. But the Royal donation of botanical, natural-historical, and economical collections of books has at once so considerably augmented the University’s stock of books, that one cannot with reason complain of an interim contribution of 100 Rix-dollars from the revenues of the Library.”

“For Prof. Fabritius, notwithstanding all the pains employed, we have been unable to discover any interim salary within the University. We therefore find ourselves constrained most humbly to petition that it may most graciously please the King to cause his annual 400 Rix-dollars for the present to be paid out of the Private Treasury.”

“Prof. Brünniche enjoys free lodging in the buildings of the Communitet, adjoining the Natural Cabinet which is to be established. Should it please His Majesty to grant to Prof. Fabritius forthwith the same advantage which the foundation of the Charlottenborg institution accords to its Professors; it might be arranged that the greater part of the rooms in that portion of the Communitet building now inhabited by the Oeconomus of the Communitet be assigned to him for that purpose. In this case, however, it would be equitable to grant the said Oeconomus an annual compensation of at least 100 Rix-dollars, since the use of that part of the building is allotted to him as part of his salary. We add that this expense might likewise be spared, if Prof. Fabritius were permitted to occupy the rooms which, upon Mr. Chardin’s departure, will shortly become vacant at Charlottenborg, until such time as means may later be found to provide him with a permanent dwelling.”

“That Prof. Fabritius in the meantime receives 100 Rix-dollars more in interim salary than Prof. Brünniche is not inequitable, inasmuch as the latter has seniority over the former, and may consequently hope sooner to attain to the full emoluments of office.”

“Lastly, it devolves upon us to set forth the prospect above indicated, according to which the two new Professors may, in due course of their seniority, obtain regular salaries from the University’s own funds, without any necessity of increasing the number of chairs in the philosophical Faculty.

This prospect consists briefly in the following: By virtue of the Royal Foundation, which constitutes the present fundamental law of the University, the philosophical Faculty possesses seven chairs for the following sciences, namely: 1) History and Geography. 2) The Oriental Languages. 3) The Greek Language. 4) The Latin Language together with Rhetoric. 5) Higher Mathematics and Astronomy. 6) Pure and Elementary Mathematics; and 7) Logic and Metaphysics.

Now we are of opinion that the presently worthily filled professio linguarum orientalium, upon a vacancy occurring, might very suitably and conveniently be transferred to a Professor of Theology, to which faculty the Oriental languages properly belong and are chiefly serviceable; and that the professio linguæ latinæ & eloquentiæ, upon a like vacancy, might very well be united with the professio græcæ linguæ, which indeed is everywhere indispensable to the former.

In this manner room would be created for two chairs in the philosophical Faculty, which might then be assigned to Natural History and Oeconomy; and their Professors would thus, in conformity with His Majesty’s will, be entirely maintained from the University’s own resources.

More, in accordance with the limits of our present commission, we dare not express, without further command, concerning the arrangements intended for the speedy advancement of this matter; but we commend the foregoing to His Majesty’s most gracious consideration, should any alteration take place in the constitution of the Universities.”

“From what precedes it appears that, having ascertained the whole sum necessary for the first establishment of this institution, the following balance presents itself with respect to the annual expenses of maintenance. The requisite expenditures are: for the enlargement of the Cabinet of Natural History 100 Rix-dollars; for the laboratorium chymicum 100 Rix-dollars; for the Model Chamber 50 Rix-dollars; for a messenger attached to the institution 50 Rix-dollars; for the interim lodging of the Professor of Oeconomy 100 Rix-dollars; for the interim salaries of both Professors 700 Rix-dollars: in total 1100 Rix-dollars.

To meet these annual charges we have likewise identified and assigned 500 Rix-dollars. Consequently there remains an overall deficit of 600 Rix-dollars, which, in conformity with the most gracious command communicated to us, is for the present to be assigned and made good from the Royal Private Treasury.”

3.



“The plan recommended to us for drafting, whereby this Institute might become most useful to the Public, divides itself naturally into three sections: the first concerning the necessary preparatory establishments; the second, the instruction and duties of the teachers; the third, the aids and encouragements which in time are to be provided for teachers and students.”

1) “In order that this noble Institute, with which the botanical establishments stand in notable connexion, may afford the benefit desired, it would be requisite

a) that the Cabinet of Natural History be duly arranged and brought into order;
b) that a laboratorium chymicum be established for the use of the Professor of historia naturalis;
c) that it would be advantageous for the botanical library to be brought together and united with the library of Natural History and Oeconomy, and that the entire collection be properly arranged and maintained for public use in a place suitably fitted for that purpose;
d) that for the Professor of Oeconomy a Model Chamber be established, without which he can scarcely produce the expected utility; and finally
e) that it be considered expedient to arrange a separate auditorium, wherein the botanical, natural-historical, and economical lectures should be delivered, which, besides being conducted in the field, the garden, the cabinet, and the laboratory, might also be held there.

For important reasons we add that it would be well if the University were given to understand that it should not sell the smaller botanical garden at Castelvej, to the sale of which it is otherwise entitled by virtue of a Royal grant.

Lastly, we are of opinion that the entire Natural History establishment ought to remain at Charlottenborg until such time as the necessary arrangements at the University are completed; the more so as there a laboratorium chymicum is already in readiness, and the transfer, if desired, might likewise take place there.”

2) “In the Instruction to be communicated, it ought in our opinion first to be determined:

A) “That both Professors here spoken of, as well as the Professor of Botany, should in their lectures have chiefly in view the practical parts of their sciences, or those branches thereof which might prove most useful to the commonwealth.
B) “That to this end they should hold their public lectures, as far as possible, in the Danish or German tongue; yet it should nevertheless be permitted to them, in private colleges, to make use of the Latin or any other language which they might prefer.
C) “That, by reason of the natural connexion of these sciences, they should from the very beginning, and thereafter, by previous agreement, arrange their lecture hours in such manner that they should never be held at the same time; in order that the lovers of these sciences, who might commonly desire to attend all their lectures, should not be compelled to neglect one hour in order to profit from another.”

“In particular, the Professor of Natural History ought to be enjoined annually to deliver, without fee, at least two public courses; namely, one upon Natural History and one upon Chemistry; and to arrange them in such a manner that the doctrines of the former might, as far as possible, be confirmed by the experiments of the latter.

In this connexion it ought expressly to be recommended that a clear and comprehensive knowledge be diffused of the most common varieties of soils and minerals found within the Royal States. Without the aid of these most necessary branches of knowledge—whose deficiency is but too frequently and grievously remarked in the Transactions of the Royal Society for Agriculture—we shall never attain to an orderly, intelligent, and truly advantageous husbandry, nor learn to know and employ to good effect the many other natural treasures with which these realms are so abundantly endowed.”

“As we presume that the establishment of a chair of Oeconomy at the University has not so much in view the higher or political Oeconomy, as rather Rural Oeconomy and its diffusion; we therefore think, in consideration of the importance and necessity of the latter, that it ought chiefly to be entrusted to the Professor of Oeconomy annually to deliver at least ten public courses pertaining thereto, without expense to the hearers.

The one, which we would term the Cursus ruralis, ought solely to be devoted to the necessary and useful science of the husbandman, and to have for its aim the formation of good and enlarged-minded farmers for the State, in which respect there is so great a deficiency. Accordingly, agriculture and meadow management, cattle-breeding, hop-cultivation, sheep- and bee-keeping—in a word, the whole science of husbandry in its full extent—ought to be taught in a regular, clear, and systematic manner; and all known advantages and practical methods diligently pointed out.

In particular, the Professor ought to be recommended to employ the most thorough knowledge of soils, to determine, according to their known constituent parts, their proper manuring, and especially to explain in detail their most suitable mixtures with one another for the fertilisation of the various kinds of ground. Finally, in these lectures he should most carefully endeavour to render his hearers sufficiently acquainted with the best implements of agriculture and rural economy, according to their construction and utility; for which purpose these ought each time to be exhibited in models and explained in all their parts.“

“The second annual, public, and free course of the Professor of Oeconomy, which we would entitle Lectures upon the Science of Industry (Gewerbewissenschaft), ought to have for its object the doctrine of trades, manufactures, factories, and other mechanical arts, whereby natural products are refined.

The primary materials, their differences, selection, and preparation, should herein be considered, together with the machines and instruments required thereto, so far as may be practicable. This instruction, greatly facilitated by the Description des arts & des métiers published by the Paris Academy of Sciences, might become yet more useful if the defects and possible improvements of our domestic trades and manufactures were diligently pointed out therein.

Moreover, these instructions ought to be concluded with solid information concerning the theory of commerce and navigation, and illustrated by frequent historical examples, for which purpose Anderson’s Commercial Chart might be employed with advantage.“

“The remaining parts of the oeconomical sciences might be left to private colleges, which would suffer too much were the Professor charged with more than the most generally useful public lectures.”

3) “In order to place the Professor of Natural History as well as the Professor of Economics in a position thoroughly to investigate the principal kinds of soil, or those most commonly found within the Royal States, and sufficiently to determine their proper treatment for the benefit of agriculture and rural economy, care must be taken that samples thereof be collected with discernment and submitted from all provinces.

To this end, carefully considered instructions ought to be issued to the officials of the realm, so that, as far as possible, ignorance or negligence may not render the agricultural theory partly founded thereupon unnecessarily defective. A beginning might herein be made with Denmark, and at first only with the marl varieties. When these important investigations have in this manner been set in motion, one might afterwards here and in the other countries conveniently, and almost without expense, proceed with the remaining soil and mineral types.

The Professor of Economics ought to be permitted, in a part of the botanical garden, to conduct experiments on a small scale with fodder plants and the like; for which purpose the above-mentioned garden by the Castle Road, which should be reserved for the University, appears particularly suitable. And the Professor of Botany should be instructed to assist him therein.”

“As the sciences which are to be brought into flourishing condition by these institutions are still rather new in this country and, as a consequence of former modes of education, will at first find but few admirers; yet since it is of the highest necessity that the generally useful parts of Natural History, Chemistry, and Botany, together with the economic sciences, be spread as rapidly as possible throughout all the King’s dominions: it would therefore be proper to consider encouraging the nation, by sufficient incentives, to cultivate these sciences.

To attain this end, we regard it as an effective measure if it were established that rural benefices should henceforth be preferentially conferred upon such candidates as, cæteris paribus, have acquired sound knowledge in the above-mentioned sciences. The same principle ought to extend to all county offices and the more important customs posts; from which, in time, besides the general advantages to the country, this further beneficial effect might be expected, namely that the Boards of Finance and Commerce, upon suitable occasions, might look for useful reports and assistance from their officials.

Candidates for all such offices should therefore be required to support their petitions with credible testimonials of their competence and of the aforementioned knowledge; and the professors who are to issue such attestations should be strictly instructed to frame them in accordance with the truth.

This, in brief, was the outline of the plan entrusted to us, which may easily be further elaborated, and by the execution of which the former Charlottenborg Institute, in its new connection with the University, may in our opinion become of great benefit to the commonwealth.

Thus we hope to have fulfilled, to the best of our ability, the most gracious Royal Order laid upon us.

Copenhagen, 7 May 1771.

Hübner.”
